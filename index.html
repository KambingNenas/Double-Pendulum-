Deriving the Chaos (The Physics of $\mathbb{R}^4$)

To avoid the stale, overused Lorenz system, we turn to classical mechanics: The Frictionless Double Pendulum. It is a brilliant example of chaotic behavior emerging from simple Newtonian rules.

1. The Euler-Lagrange Formulation

We begin by defining the positions of the two pendulum masses ($m_1, m_2$) with arm lengths ($l_1, l_2$) and angles ($\theta_1, \theta_2$) relative to the vertical axis:

$$x_1 = l_1 \sin\theta_1, \quad y_1 = -l_1 \cos\theta_1$$ $$x_2 = l_1 \sin\theta_1 + l_2 \sin\theta_2, \quad y_2 = -l_1 \cos\theta_1 - l_2 \cos\theta_2$$

The Lagrangian of the system, $L = T - V$, is formulated by substituting the time derivatives of these coordinates:

$$ L = \frac{1}{2}(m_1+m_2)l_1^2 \dot{\theta}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 l_2^2 \dot{\theta}_2^2 + m_2 l_1 l_2 \dot{\theta}_1 \dot{\theta}_2 \cos(\theta_1-\theta_2) + (m_1+m_2)g l_1 \cos\theta_1 + m_2 g l_2 \cos\theta_2 $$

2. The Equations of Motion

We apply the Euler-Lagrange equation to both angles: $\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\theta}_i}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_i} = 0$. This yields a coupled system of second-order nonlinear differential equations.

To simulate this in our phase space ($\mathbb{R}^4$), we reduce it to a system of four first-order Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) by defining angular velocities $\omega_1 = \dot{\theta}_1$ and $\omega_2 = \dot{\theta}_2$:

$$ \frac{d\theta_1}{dt} = \omega_1 $$ $$ \frac{d\theta_2}{dt} = \omega_2 $$ $$ \frac{d\omega_1}{dt} = \frac{-g(2m_1+m_2)\sin\theta_1 - m_2 g \sin(\theta_1-2\theta_2) - 2\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)m_2(\omega_2^2 l_2 + \omega_1^2 l_1\cos(\theta_1-\theta_2))}{l_1(2m_1+m_2 - m_2\cos(2\theta_1-2\theta_2))} $$ $$ \frac{d\omega_2}{dt} = \frac{2\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)(\omega_1^2 l_1(m_1+m_2) + g(m_1+m_2)\cos\theta_1 + \omega_2^2 l_2 m_2\cos(\theta_1-\theta_2))}{l_2(2m_1+m_2 - m_2\cos(2\theta_1-2\theta_2))} $$

3. The Chaotic Regime (The Stochastic Sea)

When initialized near a horizontal state ($\theta_1 \approx \pi/2, \theta_2 \approx \pi/2$), the nonlinear coupling terms dominate. The system develops a positive Lyapunov exponent, meaning trajectories that start arbitrarily close to one another will diverge exponentially fast.
